When to Look at Solutions — DSA Learning Guide
🧠 Why This Matters
Looking at solutions too early kills your problem-solving muscle.
Looking too late wastes hours and breeds frustration.
Knowing when to look is a skill in itself — and it directly impacts how fast you grow.

⏱️ The Time Rules
Easy Problems
Try for: 15–20 minutes
If you have zero ideas after 15 min → look at a hint, not the full solution
If you have some idea but are stuck on implementation → push to 25 min, then look
Medium Problems
Try for: 30–45 minutes
First 20 min → pure thinking, pseudocode, brute force attempts
Next 15–25 min → try at least one approach fully, even if wrong
After 45 min with no real progress → it's time
Hard Problems
Try for: 45–60 minutes
These are meant to challenge you — frustration is expected
If you identify the pattern/category but can't implement → give yourself more time
If you have no clue what category this even belongs to → look after 45 min guilt-free
🚦 The Decision Framework
Ask yourself these five questions before opening the solution:

Have I re-read the problem at least twice?
→ Misreading the problem is the #1 silent time waster

Have I tried brute force?
→ Even if it's O(n³), write it out — optimizing is easier than inventing

Have I drawn it out?
→ Arrays, trees, graphs — if you haven't sketched it on paper, you haven't truly tried

Have I identified what category this might be?
→ Sliding window? DP? Graph traversal? Even a wrong guess sharpens intuition

Am I going in circles?
→ If you've re-read your same wrong code 3 times with no new idea, stop

If you answered YES to all five → look at the solution without guilt.

🔍 HOW to Look at Solutions (Just as Important)
❌ What most people do
Read the full solution top to bottom
Say "oh that makes sense"
Move on
Forget it in 2 days
✅ What you should do
Read only the approach/intuition first — not the code
Close the solution
Try to implement it yourself from the idea alone
If stuck again → look at the code, but only the part you're stuck on
After solving → close everything and solve it from scratch on a blank screen
Mark it for revisit in 2 days, then 7 days, then 14 days
📊 The Honesty Matrix
Situation	Action
Never seen this pattern before	Look after 20 min — this is learning, not testing
Seen the pattern, forgot the trick	Try for 30–40 min — this is reinforcement
Know the pattern, struggling with edge cases	Push through — this is growth
Know the pattern, know the approach, just debugging	Do NOT look — this is discipline
Completely demoralized and hating DSA	Look immediately, understand it, and protect your motivation
🧭 Mindset Shifts
Looking at a solution is not failure. Banging your head for 3 hours and learning nothing IS failure.
The goal is patterns, not problems. You're not trying to solve problem #447. You're trying to recognize sliding window patterns forever.
First pass through DSA is LEARNING. Second pass is TESTING. Don't treat your first encounter with a new pattern as an exam.
Your future self benefits most when your current self understands deeply — not when your current self suffers maximally.
🔁 The Revisit Rule
If you looked at the solution, the problem is NOT done.

It goes into your revisit queue:

Day 2 → Solve again from scratch (no peeking)
Day 7 → Solve again
Day 14 → Solve again
If you nail all three → it's yours. Move on.
If you fail any → reset the cycle
🚫 Red Flags You're Looking Too Much
You look at solutions for more than 50% of problems at your level
You "understand" solutions but can't solve similar problems independently
You've been doing DSA for weeks but can't solve easy problems without help
You skip the thinking phase entirely because "it's faster to just read"
If this is you → drop down a difficulty level and rebuild your confidence with problems you CAN solve independently.

🚫 Red Flags You're Looking Too Little
You spend 2+ hours on a single medium problem regularly
You feel angry, burned out, or like you hate programming
You avoid hard problems entirely because of ego
You "solve" problems with ugly brute force but never learn optimal approaches
If this is you → give yourself permission to learn. Solutions are textbooks, not cheat sheets.
