Daily DSA Practice Plan
🎯 The Core Philosophy
Consistency beats intensity. Every single time.

Solving 2 problems daily for 6 months will make you dramatically better than solving 20 problems in one weekend and burning out.

This plan is designed for people with jobs, college, or other responsibilities. It's realistic. It's sustainable. And it works.

⏰ How Much Time Per Day
Your Situation	Daily Commitment	Problems Per Day
Working full-time	1 to 1.5 hours	1–2 problems
College student	1.5 to 2 hours	2–3 problems
Full-time preparation (no job)	3 to 4 hours	3–5 problems
Maintaining after reaching a good level	30 to 45 minutes	1 problem
Non-negotiable minimum: 1 problem per day. Every day. No zero days.

Even on bad days, tired days, "I hate this" days — open one easy problem and solve it. Protect the streak. The streak protects you.

🗓️ The Daily Session Structure
Phase 1 — Warm-Up (5–10 minutes)
Review one problem you solved yesterday
Don't re-solve it — just recall the approach in your head
Ask yourself: what pattern did it use? What was the key insight?
This is spaced repetition and it's where long-term memory is built
Phase 2 — The Main Problem (30–60 minutes)
This is the real work. One focused problem with full effort.

First 5 minutes

Read the problem twice
Identify inputs, outputs, constraints
Write down 2–3 test cases by hand including at least one edge case
Ask yourself: what category might this be?
Next 5–10 minutes

Think about brute force first — always
Estimate its time complexity
Ask: can I do better? What's the bottleneck?
Consider which pattern might apply
Next 15–30 minutes

Implement your approach
Talk through your logic as if someone is listening (even if no one is)
If stuck, revisit the "When to Look at Solutions" guide
Don't stay stuck silently — either push forward with a new idea or seek a hint
Final 5–10 minutes

Test your solution against your handwritten test cases
Analyze time and space complexity of your solution
Ask: is there a better approach? (You don't always need to implement it, but you should know it exists)
Phase 3 — Second Problem (20–40 minutes, if time allows)
This should be a different difficulty or different topic than the main problem
If your main problem was a medium, make this an easy from a different topic
If your main problem was an easy, try a medium from the same topic
This creates variety and prevents tunnel vision
Phase 4 — Reflection and Logging (5 minutes)
This is the most skipped step and the most valuable one.

Write down in your tracker:

Problem name and number
Topic and pattern used
Whether you solved it independently, needed a hint, or needed the full solution
What was the key insight or trick
Rate your confidence: "would I solve this if I saw it again in an interview?" (yes / probably / no)
If "no" or "probably" → it goes in the revisit queue
📅 The Weekly Structure
Not every day should look the same. Variety prevents burnout and builds well-rounded skills.

Monday — New Topic Day
Pick a topic you haven't practiced recently or are weakest at
Solve 1 easy + 1 medium from that topic
Focus on understanding the pattern, not speed
Read editorial explanations even if you solved it
Tuesday — Reinforcement Day
Revisit 1–2 problems from your revisit queue
Solve them from scratch without looking at anything
If you can solve them cleanly → remove from queue
If you still struggle → keep in queue and try again next week
Wednesday — Medium Grind Day
Solve 2 medium problems from different topics
Focus on solving within a time limit (35–40 minutes each)
Start building interview-like pressure
No peeking at hints — commit to an approach and see it through
Thursday — Weak Spot Day
Look at your tracker and find topics where your confidence ratings are lowest
Spend the entire session on that one topic
Even if it means solving easy problems — fill the gap
Watch one conceptual video or read one article about that topic if needed
Friday — Hard Problem Day (when ready)
Attempt one hard problem (only after you're comfortable with mediums)
Give yourself the full 45–60 minutes
It's okay to not solve it — the attempt itself builds mental muscle
Read the solution carefully and understand every line of reasoning
If you're not ready for hards yet, do two mediums instead
Saturday — Mock Interview Day
Pick 2 problems (1 easy, 1 medium OR 2 mediums)
Set a strict timer: 20 minutes for easy, 35 minutes for medium
No IDE autocomplete, no Google, no hints
Speak your thought process out loud the entire time
Grade yourself honestly: Did you communicate clearly? Did you handle edge cases? Did you finish in time?
Sunday — Rest or Light Review
Option A: Complete rest. No DSA. Recharge.
Option B: Light review — skim through your weekly log, re-read key insights, watch one conceptual video
Never grind on Sunday. Burnout is the number one reason people quit DSA prep. Protect your energy.
📊 The Topic Rotation (16-Week Cycle)
Weeks 1–2: Arrays and Strings
Traversal, manipulation, subarrays
Key patterns: two pointers, prefix sum
Target: 12–15 problems
Weeks 3–4: Hashmaps and Sets
Frequency counting, grouping, lookups
Key patterns: complement finding, anagram detection
Target: 10–12 problems
Week 5: Sliding Window
Fixed and variable window sizes
Key patterns: max/min in window, substring problems
Target: 8–10 problems
Week 6: Binary Search
Standard and modified binary search
Key patterns: search in rotated array, finding boundaries, search on answer
Target: 8–10 problems
Weeks 7–8: Linked Lists and Stacks/Queues
Reversal, cycle detection, merge operations
Monotonic stacks, valid parentheses, next greater element
Target: 12–15 problems
Weeks 9–10: Trees (Binary Trees and BSTs)
Traversals (inorder, preorder, postorder, level-order)
Key patterns: recursion, DFS, BFS on trees, path problems
Target: 15–18 problems
Week 11: Heaps and Priority Queues
Top-K problems, median finding, merge K sorted
Key patterns: min-heap vs max-heap selection
Target: 6–8 problems
Weeks 12–13: Graphs
BFS, DFS, connected components, cycle detection
Topological sort, shortest path basics
Key patterns: grid as graph, adjacency list traversal
Target: 12–15 problems
Weeks 14–15: Dynamic Programming
1D DP, 2D DP, common patterns
Key patterns: knapsack, LCS, LIS, coin change, grid paths
Target: 15–18 problems
Week 16: Backtracking and Miscellaneous
Permutations, combinations, subsets
Tries, bit manipulation, greedy
Target: 8–10 problems
After completing one cycle: repeat with harder problems from the same topics.

🔁 The Revisit System
Your revisit queue is where real learning happens. Solving a problem once means almost nothing. Solving it three times across two weeks means you own it.

Trigger	When to Revisit
Needed the full solution	Day 1 → Day 3 → Day 7 → Day 14
Needed a hint	Day 3 → Day 7 → Day 14
Solved but felt shaky	Day 7 → Day 14
Solved cleanly and confidently	No revisit needed
Revisit rules:

Always solve from a blank screen — no peeking at your old code
If you fail a revisit, reset the cycle
Revisits take priority over new problems — don't let the queue grow endlessly
Ideal revisit queue size: 10–20 problems at any time. If it's larger, slow down on new problems.
⚡ Speed Benchmarks to Aim For
These are targets to work toward over weeks and months. Don't stress about them on day one.

By Week 4
Solve most easy problems in under 15 minutes
Solve straightforward mediums in under 35 minutes
Can identify the topic/pattern for 60% of problems after reading them
By Week 8
Solve easy problems in under 10 minutes
Solve mediums in under 30 minutes consistently
Start attempting hards without complete panic
Can identify the pattern for 80% of problems
By Week 14
Easy problems feel automatic — under 8 minutes
Most mediums solved in 20–30 minutes
Can make meaningful progress on hards within 45 minutes
Can explain your approach clearly before writing any code
By Week 16+
You're interview-ready for most companies
Focus shifts to mock interviews and company-specific preparation
Maintenance mode: 1 problem per day to stay sharp
🧠 What To Do When You Feel Stuck in a Rut
This will happen. Usually around week 3–4 and again around week 8–10. It's normal.

Symptoms
Every problem feels impossible
You feel like you're not improving
You dread opening the practice platform
You start comparing yourself to others online
The Fix
Drop down in difficulty. Seriously. Go back to easy problems for 2–3 days. Rebuild your confidence. Remind yourself that you CAN solve things.

Switch topics. If graphs are destroying you, go do some hashmap problems. Come back to graphs in a week with fresh eyes.

Watch someone else solve problems. YouTube has incredible creators who walk through their thought process. Sometimes seeing HOW someone thinks unlocks something in you.

Review your tracker. Look at where you were two weeks ago. Look at problems that felt impossible then that you can solve now. Progress is real — you just can't see it day-to-day.

Talk to someone. A friend, a study group, an online community. DSA prep in isolation is hard. Shared struggle is easier.

Take a real rest day. Not a "light review" day. A real day off. Go outside. Exercise. Watch something dumb on Netflix. Come back tomorrow.

🚫 Common Daily Practice Mistakes
Mistake 1: Random problem picking
Why it's bad: No structure means no pattern recognition. You might solve 5 tree problems and 0 graph problems without realizing it.
Fix: Follow the topic rotation. Be intentional about what you practice each day.
Mistake 2: Spending 3 hours on one problem
Why it's bad: Diminishing returns after 45–60 minutes. You're building frustration, not skill.
Fix: Use the time limits. Look at the solution. Learn. Move on. Revisit later.
Mistake 3: Only solving easy problems
Why it's bad: Feels productive but you're in your comfort zone. Interviews are mediums and hards.
Fix: After week 2, at least half your problems should be mediums.
Mistake 4: Never reviewing solved problems
Why it's bad: You forget 80% of what you solved within a week. All that time is wasted.
Fix: The revisit system exists for a reason. Use it.
Mistake 5: Skipping the thinking phase
Why it's bad: If you jump straight to coding, you're training yourself to code without thinking. In interviews, the thinking IS the interview.
Fix: Force yourself to spend 5–10 minutes thinking and planning before writing a single line.
Mistake 6: Not tracking anything
Why it's bad: You have no idea where you're weak, what to revisit, or whether you're improving.
Fix: 5 minutes of logging per day. That's all it takes.
Mistake 7: Practicing without time pressure
Why it's bad: Real interviews have strict time limits. If you always practice in relaxed mode, interview pressure will destroy you.
Fix: From week 3 onward, always set a timer. At least for your main problem of the day.
🏁 The Non-Negotiable Daily Checklist
Before you close your laptop each day, make sure you can check all of these:

 Solved at least one problem
 Spent dedicated thinking time before coding
 Analyzed time and space complexity of my solution
 Logged the problem in my tracker
 Reviewed at least one previously solved problem (even just mentally)
 Identified the pattern or technique used
 Was honest with myself about whether I truly understood it
🗺️ The Big Picture
Month	Where You Should Be
Month 1	Comfortable with arrays, strings, hashmaps, basic patterns. Solving most easys independently. Starting mediums.
Month 2	Comfortable with trees, binary search, sliding window, stacks. Solving mediums regularly. Starting to see patterns across problems.
Month 3	Comfortable with graphs, heaps, basic DP. Mediums feel manageable. Attempting hards. Pattern recognition is clicking.
Month 4	Strong across all major topics. Mediums solved under time pressure. Can make progress on most hards. Ready for mock interviews.
Month 5+	Interview-ready. Fine-tuning weak areas. Doing mock interviews weekly. Company-specific preparation. Maintenance mode.
🎯 One-Liner to Remember
"One problem a day, reviewed and understood, is worth more than ten problems skimmed and forgotten."
